Sirraya Lipika Tokenizer

A Neural Audio Tokenizer for Indian Language Speech Processing
Amir Hameed Mir
Sirraya.org — Sovereign Indian AI Research
Technical Whitepaper v1.0 · March 2026
Abstract

We present the Sirraya Lipika Tokenizer, a neural audio codec specifically designed for Indian language speech processing. Building upon foundational work in neural audio compression [1,2] and semantic representation learning [5,6], Lipika introduces three novel contributions: (1) a ScriptFamilyAdapter using Adaptive Layer Normalization for language-specific conditioning, (2) fused semantic-acoustic tokens through distillation from W2V-BERT [6] with a 10× loss weighting, and (3) retroflex bias initialization for handling sounds unique to Indic phonetics. The system achieves 240× temporal compression (24 kHz → 100 Hz tokens) while preserving linguistic content through 8-layer Residual Vector Quantization [2] with EMA updates and dead-code reset [14,15]. This whitepaper provides a comprehensive technical specification of the architecture, training methodology, and design decisions underlying the Lipika Tokenizer.

1. Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

Neural audio codecs have emerged as a critical component in modern speech processing systems, particularly for Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis. Systems like VALL-E [4] and AudioLM [16] demonstrate that discrete audio tokens can serve as an effective representation for language models to generate natural speech. However, existing tokenizers such as SoundStream [2] and EnCodec [1] are designed primarily for English and other Western languages, with limited consideration for the phonetic diversity of Indian languages.

The absence of an Indian-language-specific audio tokenizer creates a dependency on foreign AI infrastructure and fails to capture critical phonetic distinctions—such as retroflexion, aspiration, and vowel length—that are phonemic in Indic languages. This whitepaper introduces the Sirraya Lipika Tokenizer to address this gap.

1.2 Unique Challenges of Indian Languages

Indian languages present several phonetic features that are absent or non-contrastive in English:

Feature Description Example (Hindi) Acoustic Challenge
Retroflexion Consonants produced with tongue curled back ट (ṭa) vs त (ta) High-frequency energy (8-12 kHz) distinguishes place of articulation
Aspiration Consonants with breathy release ख (kha) vs क (ka) Voice onset time and spectral tilt differentiate aspirated/unaspirated pairs
Vowel Length Phonemic duration contrast अ (a) vs आ (ā) Temporal precision required; affects meaning
Schwa Deletion Inherent vowel deletion in specific contexts कम (kam) vs काम (kām) Context-dependent pronunciation rules
Script Diversity 12 major script families Devanagari, Tamil, Bengali, etc. Different writing systems correlate with phonetic inventories

These features require a tokenizer that can allocate representational capacity to fine-grained acoustic distinctions while maintaining linguistic invariance across speakers and recording conditions.

1.3 Design Goals

The Lipika Tokenizer is designed with the following objectives:

  1. Linguistic Fidelity: Preserve phonemic contrasts critical for Indian languages
  2. Semantic Alignment: Ensure first token captures phoneme identity via distillation from W2V-BERT
  3. Language Adaptability: Condition on script family to anticipate phonetic characteristics
  4. High Compression: Achieve 240× temporal reduction (24 kHz → 100 Hz tokens)
  5. Production Readiness: Support distributed training, mixed precision, and inference APIs
  6. Reproducibility: Open-source implementation with comprehensive documentation

The Lipika Tokenizer follows the encoder-quantizer-decoder paradigm established by SoundStream [2] and EnCodec [1], with three key modifications: (1) script-family conditioning via AdaLN, (2) semantic distillation on the first codebook, and (3) retroflex bias initialization.

2.2 Design Philosophy

The architecture embodies several design principles drawn from the literature:

  • Causality: All convolutions are causal, enabling streaming applications and preventing future information leakage. This follows the design of WaveNet [17] and is essential for real-time TTS.
  • Residual Learning: Each codebook quantizes the residual of previous layers, allowing hierarchical representation (coarse → fine). This follows SoundStream [2] and enables efficient use of multiple codebooks.
  • EMA Codebook Updates: Codebooks are updated via exponential moving average rather than gradient descent, following AudioCraft [14] and VQ-VAE [3] best practices for training stability.
  • Dead Code Reset: Codebook entries used fewer than 2 times per batch are reinitialized, preventing collapse [15].
  • AdaLN Conditioning: Script-family information is injected via Adaptive Layer Normalization [12], modulating all features rather than just early layers.

3. Core Components

3.1 AudioEncoder

The AudioEncoder compresses raw audio waveforms into a compact latent representation using a stack of causal convolutional blocks. The design follows EnCodec [1] with modifications for 24 kHz sampling.

Architecture Details

Layer Input Channels Output Channels Stride Temporal Resolution
Stem (kernel=7) 1 512 1 24,000 Hz
EncoderBlock 1 512 1024 2 12,000 Hz
EncoderBlock 2 1024 2048 4 3,000 Hz
EncoderBlock 3 2048 4096 5 600 Hz
EncoderBlock 4 4096 8192 6 100 Hz
Bottleneck (1×1 conv) 8192 512 1 100 Hz

Each EncoderBlock contains three residual blocks with dilations [1,3,9] followed by a strided convolution. The dilation schedule ensures exponential receptive field growth with linear parameter increase, following WaveNet [17].

Mathematical Formulation:
x₀ = Conv1d_stem(waveform)
xᵢ = EncoderBlockᵢ(xᵢ₋₁) for i = 1..4
z = LayerNorm(Bottleneck(x₄))

where EncoderBlock(x) = AvgPool(Conv1d(ResBlock₃(ResBlock₂(ResBlock₁(x)))))

Retroflex Bias Initialization

Scripts with retroflex consonants receive a bias in the first 8 dimensions of their embeddings:

# Retroflex bias initialization for sf_id in RETROFLEX_SCRIPTS: self.embed.weight[sf_id, :8] += 0.5

This soft inductive bias encourages the network to allocate capacity for high-frequency analysis when processing retroflex-rich languages. The bias is trainable and can be corrected by the optimization process if inappropriate.

AdaLN Formulation

e = Embedding(script_id)   (B, 64)
c = Proj(e)   (B, 512)
scale = scale_head(c)   (B, 512)
shift = shift_head(c)   (B, 512)
z_norm = LayerNorm(z)
z_cond = z_norm × (1 + scale) + shift

The scale and shift are broadcast over the time dimension, applying the same conditioning to every frame of the latent sequence.

3.3 ResidualVectorQuantizer

The ResidualVectorQuantizer implements multi-layer residual quantization following SoundStream [2]. It projects encoder outputs to codebook dimension and applies 8 codebooks in sequence.

Architecture

Component Dimension Description
Input Projection 512 → 128 Linear projection to codebook dimension
Codebooks (×8) 1024 × 128 8 independent VectorQuantizerEMA modules
Semantic Head 128 → 256 → 1024 MLP predicting W2V-BERT features from first codebook
Output Projection 128 → 512 Linear projection back to decoder dimension

Quantization Algorithm

# Input: z (B, T, 512) z_proj = input_proj(z) # (B, T, 128) residual = z_proj z_q_total = 0 codes = [] for i, vq in enumerate(codebooks): z_q_i, indices_i, loss_i = vq(residual) if i == 0 and w2v_targets is not None: pred = semantic_head(z_q_i) semantic_loss = MSE(pred, w2v_targets) z_q_total += z_q_i residual = residual - z_q_i.detach() codes.append(indices_i) total_vq_loss += loss_i # Output z_q_out = output_proj(z_q_total) # (B, T, 512) all_codes = stack(codes, dim=-1) # (B, T, 8)

The residual connection ensures that later codebooks only need to encode information not captured by earlier ones, promoting specialization and efficient use of capacity.

3.4 VectorQuantizerEMA

Each VectorQuantizerEMA implements a single codebook with exponential moving average updates and dead-code reset. This follows best practices from VQ-VAE [3] and AudioCraft [14].

Core Data Structures

Buffer Shape Purpose
embedding (1024, 128) The actual codebook entries
cluster_size (1024,) EMA of usage counts
embed_avg (1024, 128) EMA of summed vectors per entry

Distance Computation

Efficient squared L2 distance computation using the expansion:

||z - e||² = ||z||² - 2⟨z, e⟩ + ||e||²

This vectorized computation avoids explicit loops over codebook entries.

EMA Update

# Update EMA statistics cluster_size = decay * cluster_size + (1-decay) * counts embed_avg = decay * embed_avg + (1-decay) * embed_sum # Laplace smoothing n = cluster_size.sum() smoothed = (cluster_size + epsilon) / (n + K*epsilon) * n # Update codebook embedding = embed_avg / smoothed.unsqueeze(1)

Dead Code Reset

Codebook entries with usage below threshold_ema_dead_code = 2 are reset:

dead_mask = counts < threshold_dead if dead_mask.any(): n_dead = dead_mask.sum() perm = torch.randperm(n_live)[:n_dead] embedding[dead_mask] = flat_z[perm] embed_avg[dead_mask] = flat_z[perm] cluster_size[dead_mask] = threshold_dead

This prevents codebook collapse by reinitializing dead entries with random samples from the current batch.

3.5 SemanticTeacher (W2V-BERT)

The SemanticTeacher is a frozen W2V-BERT-2.0 model [6] that provides linguistic feature targets for distillation. It has 315M parameters and 24 transformer layers.

Configuration

Parameter Value Rationale
Model facebook/w2v-bert-2.0 State-of-the-art self-supervised speech model
Hidden Layer 6 (0-indexed) Empirically optimal for phone-level features [18]
Target SR 16 kHz W2V-BERT pretrained at 16 kHz
Output Dimension 1024 Hidden size of W2V-BERT

The teacher is completely frozen during training (requires_grad = False) and runs in eval() mode to ensure stable targets.

3.6 AudioDecoder

The AudioDecoder mirrors the encoder, using transposed convolutions to upsample quantized latents back to waveform.

Architecture

Layer Input Channels Output Channels Stride Temporal Resolution
Entry (kernel=7) 512 512 1 100 Hz
DecoderBlock 1 512 256 6 600 Hz
DecoderBlock 2 256 128 5 3,000 Hz
DecoderBlock 3 128 64 4 12,000 Hz
DecoderBlock 4 64 32 2 24,000 Hz
Output (kernel=7, Tanh) 32 1 1 24,000 Hz

Each DecoderBlock contains a transposed convolution followed by three residual blocks with dilations [1,3,9]. The transposed convolution is causal (padding removed from right) to maintain streaming capability.

3.7 MultiScaleMultiPeriodDiscriminator

The discriminator combines multi-scale [7] and multi-period [8] discriminators for adversarial training. This follows HiFi-GAN [8] and has been shown to substantially improve perceptual quality.

Scale 1 Identity 24,000 Hz Fine details, high-frequency artifacts
Scale 2 AvgPool1d(2) 12,000 Hz Medium-scale patterns, formants
Scale 3 AvgPool1d(4) 6,000 Hz Coarse structure, prosody

Multi-Period Discriminators (×5)

5(B, 1, T/5, 5)Syllable-level rhythm
7(B, 1, T/7, 7)Word-level prosody
11(B, 1, T/11, 11)Phrase-level intonation

All discriminator convolutions use spectral normalization [10] to stabilize GAN training.

4. Loss Functions

Lipika employs a multi-component loss function following EnCodec [1] and HiFi-GAN [8], with an additional semantic distillation term weighted at 10×.

4.1 Reconstruction Losses

Time-Domain L1 Loss

L₁ = ||ŷ - y||₁

Weight: λ₁ = 0.1

Mel-Spectrogram Loss

L_mel = ||log(Mel(ŷ)) - log(Mel(y))||₁

Using 128 mel bands from 0-12 kHz, with log compression to approximate human loudness perception. Weight: λ_mel = 1.0

Multi-Scale STFT Loss

L_stft = Σ_{n∈[256,512,1024,2048]} [ ||S_n(ŷ) - S_n(y)||_F / ||S_n(y)||_F + ||log(1+S_n(ŷ)) - log(1+S_n(y))||₁ ]

Weight: λ_stft = 1.0

4.2 Vector Quantization Loss

L_vq = β · ||sg(z_q) - z||₂²

Where β = 0.25 is the commitment cost [3] and sg denotes stop-gradient. Note that codebook loss is handled by EMA updates rather than gradient descent.

4.3 Semantic Distillation Loss

L_sem = ||φ(z_q¹) - ψ(y)||₂²

Where z_q¹ is the first codebook output, φ is the semantic head MLP, and ψ is the frozen W2V-BERT teacher. Weight: λ_sem = 10.0 (highest among all losses)

Rationale for λ_sem = 10.0: The semantic loss must dominate to force the first codebook to prioritize linguistic content over acoustic details. Without this high weight, the model would optimize for reconstruction quality at the expense of semantic meaning, producing tokens that are speaker-dependent rather than phoneme-invariant.

4.4 Adversarial Losses

Generator Adversarial Loss (Hinge)

L_adv = -𝔼[D(ŷ)]

Weight: λ_adv = 3.0

Feature Matching Loss

L_fm = Σ_l ||D_l(ŷ) - D_l(y)||₁

Weight: λ_fm = 3.0

Discriminator Loss

L_D = 𝔼[max(0, 1 - D(y))] + 𝔼[max(0, 1 + D(ŷ))]

Used only for discriminator updates, not for generator.

4.5 Total Loss Formulation

L_total = 0.1·L₁ + 1.0·L_mel + 1.0·L_stft + 1.0·L_vq + 10.0·L_sem + 3.0·L_adv + 3.0·L_fm

This weighting scheme balances multiple objectives: basic accuracy (L₁), perceptual quality (L_mel, L_stft), quantization stability (L_vq), linguistic content (L_sem), and naturalness (L_adv, L_fm).

5.2 Learning Rate Scheduling

Cosine decay with linear warmup:

η(t) = η₀ · \begin{cases} t / t_{warm} & \text{if } t < t_{warm} \\ η_{min} + (1 - η_{min}) · \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos(π·(t - t_{warm})/(t_{decay} - t_{warm}))) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}

Where t_warm = 1000 steps, t_decay = 400,000 steps, and η_min = 0.1·η₀.

5.3 Codebook Health Monitoring

The CodebookMonitor tracks two key metrics:

Metric Definition Healthy Range Action if Unhealthy
Usage Percentage Fraction of codebook entries used ≥ once in last 100 steps > 80% Increase learning rate or check data diversity
Perplexity exp(entropy) = exp(-∑ p_i log p_i) > 500 (for 1024 entries) Check for mode collapse; reset dead codes if needed

A collapse warning is raised if any codebook usage falls below 20%.

5.4 Distributed Training

Lipika supports Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) training with NCCL backend. Key considerations:

  • Codebook Synchronization: EMA counts and sums are all-reduced across GPUs to maintain identical codebooks
  • Mixed Precision: Automatic bfloat16/float16 scaling with GradScaler
  • Gradient Accumulation: Configurable steps for effective batch size scaling

6.2 Language Metadata

Each audio file may have a sidecar JSON file with the same stem containing:

{ "lang": "hi" # ISO 639-1 language code }

The language code maps to script family via LANG_TO_SCRIPT mapping. If no metadata is found, Devanagari (Hindi) is assumed.

6.3 Preprocessing Pipeline

  1. Load audio at original sample rate
  2. Convert to mono (average channels)
  3. Resample to 24 kHz if necessary
  4. Random crop to max_duration seconds
  5. Peak normalize to [-0.98, 0.98] (headroom)
  6. Convert to tensor (B, 1, T)

7. Inference

7.1 Encoding: Audio → Tokens

def encode(waveform, lang="hi"): # waveform: (1, T) at 24 kHz script_id = LANG_TO_SCRIPT[lang] cond = script_adapter(script_id) z = encoder(waveform, cond) out = rvq(z, w2v_targets=None) return out["codes"] # (1, T/240, 8)

For 1 second of audio:
Input: 24,000 samples
Output: 100 frames × 8 tokens = 800 integers (0-1023)
Storage: 800 × 10 bits = 8,000 bits = 1 KB
Compression ratio: 48:1 (assuming 16-bit input)

7.2 Decoding: Tokens → Audio

def decode(codes): # codes: (1, T_frames, 8) z_q = rvq.decode_from_codes(codes) waveform = decoder(z_q) return waveform # (1, 24000) at 24 kHz

9. Discussion

9.1 Current Limitations

  1. Teacher Dependence: The system requires the W2V-BERT teacher (315M parameters) during training, increasing computational requirements and memory footprint.
  2. Limited Tonal Language Support: Languages like Punjabi with tonal contrasts may not be fully captured by the current architecture.
  3. Evaluation Scope: Preliminary results are based on internal test sets; standardized benchmarks across all 12 script families are needed.
  4. Production Readiness: While the codebase is robust, it has not yet been deployed in production environments; performance optimization for edge devices is ongoing.

9.2 Future Work

  1. Teacher Distillation: Explore distillation from W2V-BERT to a smaller student model to reduce training requirements.
  2. Codebook Optimization: Investigate adaptive codebook sizes per language based on phonetic complexity.
  3. Tonal Language Support: Extend the architecture to explicitly model pitch contours for tonal Indian languages.
  4. Multilingual Pre-training: Pre-train on all 12 script families simultaneously to enable zero-shot transfer to low-resource languages.
  5. Hardware Acceleration: Optimize for mobile and edge deployment via quantization and pruning.

10. Conclusion

The Sirraya Lipika Tokenizer represents a significant step toward sovereign Indian AI infrastructure. By combining established neural audio codec architectures with innovations specific to Indian language phonetics—script-family conditioning, retroflex bias, and heavily weighted semantic distillation—Lipika produces discrete audio tokens that preserve linguistically meaningful distinctions while achieving high compression ratios.

This whitepaper has provided a comprehensive technical specification of the system's architecture, components, loss functions, and training methodology. The implementation is open-source and available for research and development use. We invite the Indian AI community to build upon this foundation, extending it to new languages and applications.

The journey toward AI sovereignty requires foundational components built for Indian languages by Indian researchers. Lipika is one such component—a tokenizer that understands the difference between क and ख, between त and ट, and preserves these distinctions for the TTS systems of tomorrow.
